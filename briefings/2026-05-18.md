## Domestic US Security

A CISA contractor inadvertently exposed highly privileged AWS GovCloud credentials and internal system secrets in a public GitHub repository, in what security experts described as one of the most egregious government data leaks in recent history. The repository, named "Private-CISA" and maintained by a contractor linked to government services firm Nightwing, remained publicly accessible until mid-May 2026 and contained plaintext passwords, API tokens, and administrative credentials to at least three AWS GovCloud servers, as well as access to CISA's internal software artifact repository. CISA stated there is currently no indication that sensitive data was actively compromised, but the incident raised serious questions about contractor security hygiene and oversight, particularly given that the contractor had explicitly disabled GitHub's default secret-blocking settings. Separately, CISA had added a critical-severity Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN authentication bypass vulnerability (CVE-2026-20182, CVSS 10.0) to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on May 14, with a federal remediation deadline of May 17; the flaw, attributed to threat actor UAT-8616, allows unauthenticated remote attackers to seize full administrative control of SD-WAN infrastructure and has been exploited in the wild since at least 2023. The twin incidents underscore growing pressure on CISA as it continues to recover from a record 75-day federal shutdown that degraded the agency's capacity for proactive critical infrastructure defense. CISA's newly launched CI Fortify initiative, which urges critical infrastructure operators to develop isolation and recovery capabilities capable of sustaining operations for "weeks to months" during a conflict or cyberattack, reflects the agency's escalating concern about nation-state threats from Chinese groups Volt Typhoon and Salt Typhoon.

## China / Taiwan

Taiwan's Ministry of National Defense reported on May 17 that 5 sorties of PLA aircraft and 7 PLAN vessels operated in the waters and airspace around Taiwan in a 24-hour period, with 4 of the 5 sorties crossing the Taiwan Strait median line and entering Taiwan's northern and southwestern ADIZ — prompting the deployment of CAP aircraft, Navy ships, and coastal missile systems in response. The activity followed the conclusion of the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing (May 13-15), after which President Trump told Fox News he was "not looking to have somebody go independent" and questioned U.S. willingness to fight a war "9,500 miles away," while indicating arms sales to Taiwan were being "held in abeyance" as negotiating leverage with Beijing. President Lai Ching-te pushed back on May 17 via Facebook, asserting that Taiwan "will not be sacrificed or traded," that the ROC and PRC are "not subordinate to each other," and that Taiwan's sovereignty "cannot be violated or annexed" — framing the island as already de facto independent rather than pursuing formal independence. Trump's remarks generated sharp domestic political divisions in Taiwan, with the KMT opposition calling them "a heavy blow to the DPP and Lai Ching-te" and urging abandonment of pro-independence posturing, while analysts described the statements as tactical signaling to Beijing rather than a fundamental policy shift. On the defense front, Taiwan's military has been advancing plans to deploy 111 HIMARS systems armed with ATACMS missiles to outlying islands including Penghu and Dongyin, which officials describe as creating a "dead zone" to prevent PLA force concentration ahead of any amphibious operation. The Taiwan Coast Guard separately expelled PRC research vessel *Tongji* for conducting unauthorized hydrological surveys in Taiwanese waters, an activity consistent with PLA intelligence-gathering for submarine and amphibious planning.
