## Domestic US Security

On June 10, 2026, CISA issued Binding Operational Directive 26-04 (BOD 26-04), requiring all federal civilian executive branch agencies to overhaul their vulnerability remediation practices using a risk-based prioritization framework. The directive evaluates vulnerabilities across four factors — internet exposure, Known Exploited Vulnerability (KEV) status, exploit automation potential, and post-exploitation impact — and mandates that the highest-risk flaws be patched within three calendar days. CISA cited the accelerating pace of AI-assisted vulnerability weaponization as a key driver, a significant departure from prior flat-deadline patching requirements. Concurrently, DHS Science and Technology Directorate announced it had advanced foundational cybersecurity research for space systems through the Aerospace SPARTA framework, publishing prioritized countermeasures designed to harden the growing civilian and critical infrastructure dependence on satellite-based services. The directive arrives as CISA continues to operate under significant institutional strain, having shed roughly one-third of its workforce — approximately 1,000 employees — due to budget cuts, raising concerns within the security community about the agency's capacity to enforce compliance and sustain partnerships. Both actions underscore a broader federal push to tighten critical infrastructure defenses amid persistent threats from China, Russia, and Iran-affiliated actors.

## China / Taiwan

On June 10, Taiwan's military conducted its first-ever live-fire HIMARS exercise aimed into the Taiwan Strait, launching 32 U.S.-supplied rockets from the western coastline near Taichung — a section of shoreline that defense planners identify as a likely PLA amphibious landing zone. The drill also involved 155mm Paladin howitzers and domestically produced Thunderbolt-2000 rocket launchers, simulating a rapid response to an amphibious invasion scenario. The exercise marked a deliberate escalation in signal: previous HIMARS tests had been conducted on Taiwan's eastern coast, away from China; firing westward into the strait directly demonstrated strike reach toward Fujian province. President Lai Ching-te's government framed the drill as part of Taiwan's asymmetric warfare strategy — emphasizing mobile, survivable precision-strike platforms over symmetric force-matching — consistent with the island's recently passed $24.8 billion, eight-year special defense budget. This comes against a backdrop of reduced but variable PLA ADIZ incursion rates in early 2026 and an uptick in PLA Eastern Theater Command joint combat readiness patrols in May, suggesting Beijing is calibrating pressure rather than seeking immediate escalation. China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs had not issued a formal response to the HIMARS drill as of press reporting on June 10–11, though Beijing has previously condemned such U.S.-origin weapons demonstrations as "provocative."
